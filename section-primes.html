Section 3.2 Prime Numbers

Definition 3.2.1.

A positive integer \(p\) is called prime if the only positive factors of \(p\) are \(1 \and p\) itself.

A positive integer which is not prime is called \(composite\)

Note 3.2.2.

1 is neither prime nor composite. It's called a unit.

Theorem 3.2.3. The Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic.

Every integer greater than 1 can be written as a product of primes, and this product is unique up to the ordering of the prime factors.

Example 3.2.4.

Find the prime factorization of the following:

  1. 124
  2. 81
  3. 941
Answer/Video
Theorem 3.2.5.

If \(n\) is a composite number, then \(n\) has a prime divisor less than or equal to \(\sqrt{n}\text{.}\)

Proof.

Assume, to the contrary, that every prime divisor of \(n\) is greater than \(\sqrt{n}\text{.}\) Since \(n\) is composite, it has at least two prime factors, \(p_1, p_2\) so that \(n = p_1 p_2 b\) where \(b\) is positive integer. But since \(p_1 \and p_2\) are a prime divisors of \(n\text{,}\) they are necessarily larger than \(\sqrt{n}\text{.}\) So:

\begin{equation*} n = p_1 p_2 b \gt \sqrt{n} \cdot \sqrt{n} \cdot b = nb \end{equation*}

But since \(n\) is composite and \(b\in \Z^+\text{,}\) \(n\gt nb\) is a contradiction.

Example 3.2.6. Brute Force Trial Division.

To determine if a number \(n\) is prime, one only needs to test all possible prime divisors up to \(\sqrt{n}\text{.}\)

Use trial division to determine that 251 is prime.

Answer/Video
Example 3.2.7. Sieve of Eratosthenes.

In order to generate a list of all prime numbers less than or equal to \(n\text{,}\) start with a tabular listing of integers from 1 to \(n\text{:}\)

  1. Cross off the number 1.

  2. Continuing until you reach \(\sqrt{n}\text{:}\)

    1. Circle the next, non-crossed out number. It's prime.
    2. Cross out every multiple of this number.
    3. Repeat.

Run the Sieve on this table of integers from 1 to 100.

Exercises 3.2.1 Exercises

1.

Find the prime factorization of each of the following integers:

  1. 39
  2. 125
  3. 101
  4. 143
  5. 289
  6. 899
Solution
  1. \(\displaystyle 39 = 3\cdot 13\)
  2. \(\displaystyle 125 = 5^3 \)
  3. \(101\) is prime - trial divide by 2, 3, 5, 7
  4. \(\displaystyle 143 = 11\cdot 13 \)
  5. \(\displaystyle 289 = 17^2\)
  6. \(\displaystyle 899 = 29 \cdot 31\)
2.

Find the prime factorization of \(10!\) (10 factorial, not “yay ten!”)

Solution

Recall \(10! = 10 \cdot 9 \cdot 8 \cdot 7 \cdot \dots \cdot 2 \cdot 1\text{.}\) So we can do the following:

\begin{align*} 10! \amp = 10\cdot 9 \cdot 8 \cdot 7\cdot 6 \cdot 5 \cdot 4 \ cdot 3 \cdot 2 \cdot 1 \\ \amp = (2\cdot 5)\cdot 3^2 \cdot 2^3 \cdot 7\cdot (2\cdot 3) \cdot 5 \cdot 2^2 \ cdot 3 \cdot 2 \cdot 1 \\ \amp = 7^1 \cdot 5^2 \cdot 3^4 \cdot 2^8 \end{align*}
3.

Prove that every prime number greater than 3 is either one more or one less than a multiple of 6.

Hint

Prove the contrapositive by cases.

Solution

This question is of the form “if \(n\) is a prime number greater than three, then either \(n=6k+1\) or \(n=6k-1\) for some integer \(k\)”.

Proof.

(by contraposition). Assume that \(n\) is not a number of the form \(n=6k+1\) or \(n=6k-1\) for some integer \(k\text{.}\) This gives us four total cases:

Case 1: Assume \(n=6k\) for some integer \(k\text{.}\) Then \(n\) is not a prime number, since \(n\) is divisible by both 2 and 3.

Case 2: Assume \(n = 6k+2 = 2(3k+1)\) for some integer \(k\text{.}\) Then \(n\) is divisible by 2 and is hence not prime.

Case 3: assume \(n = 6k+3 = 3(2k+1)\) for some integer \(k\text{.}\) then \(n\) is divisible by 3 and is hence not prime.

Case 4: assume \(n = 6k+4 = 2(3k+2)\) for some integer \(k\text{.}\) then \(n\) is divisible by 2 and is hence not prime.

Thus if \(n\) is not of the form \(n=6k+1\) or \(n=6k-1\) for some integer \(k\text{,}\) \(n\) is not prime. By contraposition, if \(n\) is a prime greater than three then \(n\) is of the form either \(n=6k+1\) or \(n=6k-1\) for some integer \(k\text{.}\)
